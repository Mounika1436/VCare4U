Common Mental Health Disorders

Mental health disorders are common, affecting many individuals worldwide. Understanding these conditions is vital for proper treatment and support. Here are some common disorders:

Depression

Depression is a serious mood disorder causing symptoms that affect how a person feels, thinks, and handles daily activities.

  • Persistent sadness or low mood
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in activities
  • Changes in appetite or weight
  • Sleep disturbances (insomnia or oversleeping)
  • Fatigue or loss of energy
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Difficulties concentrating or making decisions
  • Restlessness or slowed movements
  • Thoughts of death or suicide
  • Physical symptoms without a clear cause

Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety is a feeling of worry or fear. It’s natural, but excessive anxiety can interfere with daily life.

  • Excessive worry or anxiety about various aspects of life
  • Restlessness or feeling on edge
  • Fatigue or difficulty concentrating
  • Irritability
  • Muscle tension
  • Sleep disturbances (difficulty falling or staying asleep)
  • Rapid heartbeat or palpitations
  • Shortness of breath or feeling smothered
  • Feeling dizzy, lightheaded, or faint
  • Avoidance of certain situations or places

Eating Disorder

An eating disorder is a mental health condition that affects eating behaviors and can result in serious health problems.

  • Severe restriction of food intake
  • Excessive preoccupation with weight or body shape
  • Binge eating or purging behaviors
  • Extreme fear of gaining weight
  • Constant dieting or avoidance of certain foods
  • Loss of interest in eating or extreme hunger
  • Excessive exercise
  • Distorted body image
  • Fatigue, dizziness, or fainting
  • Health complications like digestive issues or irregular heart rate

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is a mental health condition where a person experiences persistent, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) and engages in repetitive behaviors (compulsions) to alleviate anxiety.

  • Recurrent, intrusive, or unwanted thoughts (obsessions)
  • Repetitive behaviors or mental acts (compulsions)
  • Compulsions performed to reduce anxiety (e.g., washing hands, checking locks)
  • Excessive concern with cleanliness or symmetry
  • Fear of contamination or harm
  • Difficulty controlling or stopping compulsive behaviors
  • Spending a significant amount of time on compulsions
  • Interference with daily life or relationships
  • Distress caused by obsessions or compulsions

Phobia

Phobia is an intense, irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that poses little or no actual danger.

  • Intense fear or anxiety triggered by a specific object or situation
  • Rapid heartbeat, sweating, and shortness of breath when exposed to the feared object
  • Avoidance of situations where the feared object might be present
  • Difficulty functioning in daily life due to fear
  • Feeling overwhelmed or powerless in the presence of the phobic stimulus

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a chronic brain disorder that affects a person’s thoughts, emotions, and behavior, often causing them to lose touch with reality.

  • Hallucinations (hearing voices, seeing things that aren't there)
  • Delusions (false beliefs, such as believing one is being persecuted)
  • Disorganized speech and thinking
  • Severe mood swings
  • Lack of motivation or emotional expression
  • Withdrawal from social interactions
  • Difficulty with daily tasks or maintaining personal hygiene

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity.

  • Inattention (e.g., difficulty focusing, forgetfulness)
  • Impulsivity (e.g., difficulty waiting, interrupting others)
  • Hyperactivity (e.g., fidgeting, inability to stay seated)
  • Difficulty completing tasks or following instructions
  • Frequent mood swings or frustration
  • Disorganization in daily activities
  • Difficulty with time management

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder causes extreme mood swings that include emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression).

  • Extreme mood swings between mania and depression
  • Increased energy, activity, and impulsivity during manic episodes
  • Feelings of sadness, hopelessness, or worthlessness during depressive episodes
  • Difficulty maintaining relationships or employment
  • Changes in sleep patterns
  • Risky behavior during manic phases (e.g., spending sprees, substance abuse)

Dementia

Dementia is a general term for a decline in cognitive ability, affecting memory, thinking, and behavior to the extent that it interferes with daily life.

  • Memory loss and forgetfulness
  • Difficulty with communication and finding words
  • Confusion and disorientation
  • Difficulty with planning or organizing tasks
  • Changes in mood and behavior
  • Withdrawal from social activities
  • Difficulty performing everyday tasks

Learning Disorder

Learning disorders involve difficulties in reading, writing, or math, despite having average or above-average intelligence.

  • Difficulty understanding or applying mathematical concepts
  • Struggles with reading fluency and comprehension
  • Challenges with spelling and writing
  • Poor organizational skills
  • Difficulty following multi-step instructions
  • Delayed speech or language development

Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

Dissociative Identity Disorder involves the presence of two or more distinct personality states or identities, often triggered by trauma.

  • Presence of multiple identities with distinct behaviors, memories, and attitudes
  • Memory gaps or lost time
  • Confusion about personal identity
  • Feeling detached from oneself or one's surroundings
  • Frequent mood swings
  • Flashbacks or re-experiencing traumatic events

Autistic Disorder

Autistic disorder, also known as Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), is a developmental disorder that affects communication, behavior, and social interactions.

  • Difficulty with communication (verbal and non-verbal)
  • Difficulty understanding social cues or forming relationships
  • Repetitive behaviors or routines
  • Highly focused interests or activities
  • Sensitivity to sensory stimuli (e.g., light, sound)
  • Challenges with motor coordination
